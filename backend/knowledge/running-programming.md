# Running Programming

> Module for ZEV AI coaching knowledge base

## Why Aerobic Base Matters

Every strong runner is built on an aerobic foundation. Base building is the phase where you develop your body's ability to use oxygen efficiently, strengthen connective tissues, and build the capillary networks that deliver blood to working muscles. Without a solid base, speed work and race-specific training will break you down instead of building you up.

A proper base-building phase lasts 4-8 weeks (longer for true beginners) and consists primarily of easy, conversational-pace running. During this phase, you should be able to hold a full conversation while running. If you can't talk in complete sentences, you're going too fast. This feels painfully slow at first, but it's building the engine that powers everything else.

## The 80/20 Rule

Research consistently shows that the most effective endurance training follows an 80/20 intensity distribution: roughly 80% of your training volume at low intensity (easy effort) and 20% at moderate-to-high intensity (tempo, intervals, race pace). This is sometimes called polarized training.

Studies confirm that this distribution improves VO2 max, supports better recovery between hard sessions, reduces injury risk, and produces more consistent performance gains over time. The key insight is that most runners go too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days, ending up in a "moderate intensity rut" that produces mediocre results and excessive fatigue.

The 80/20 split is a population optimum — it works for most runners, though individual variation exists. The practical takeaway: if you run 5 days a week, 4 of those should feel genuinely easy.

## Types of Runs

**Easy Runs** — The backbone of your program. Zone 1-2 heart rate, conversational pace. These build aerobic fitness, promote recovery, and should make up the bulk of your weekly mileage.

**Long Runs** — Your longest run of the week, typically 1.5-2x your average daily distance. Run at easy pace. Builds endurance, teaches your body to burn fat, and develops mental toughness. Increase distance by no more than 10% per week.

**Tempo Runs** — Sustained effort at "comfortably hard" pace, typically zone 3-4. Usually 20-40 minutes at a pace you could hold for about an hour in a race. Improves your lactate threshold — the pace above which fatigue accumulates rapidly.

**Intervals** — Repeated hard efforts (zone 4-5) with recovery periods. Examples: 6x800m at 5K pace with 2-minute recovery jogs, or 4x1 mile at 10K pace with 3-minute rests. Builds VO2 max and running economy.

**Recovery Runs** — Shorter and slower than easy runs. Done the day after a hard session. The purpose is active recovery, not fitness building. Keep these genuinely gentle.

## Heart Rate Zones for Running

| Zone | % of Max HR | Feel | Purpose |
|------|-------------|------|---------|
| 1 | 50-60% | Very easy, walking pace | Active recovery |
| 2 | 60-70% | Easy, conversational | Aerobic base, fat burning |
| 3 | 70-80% | Moderate, can speak in short phrases | Tempo, lactate threshold |
| 4 | 80-90% | Hard, only a few words at a time | VO2 max intervals |
| 5 | 90-100% | All-out, cannot speak | Sprint/anaerobic capacity |

Most of your training should live in zones 1-2. Zone 3 is sometimes called the "gray zone" — hard enough to create fatigue but not intense enough to produce the adaptations you get from true high-intensity work. Avoid spending too much unstructured time here.

## Building Up to a Half Marathon or Marathon

**Half Marathon (12-16 weeks):**
- Prerequisite: Comfortable running 15-20 miles per week with a longest run of 6-8 miles
- Weeks 1-4: Build base, long run progresses from 7 to 9 miles
- Weeks 5-8: Introduce tempo runs, long run reaches 10-12 miles
- Weeks 9-11: Peak training, long run hits 12-14 miles, add race-pace segments
- Weeks 12 (or 12-16): Taper and race

**Marathon (16-20 weeks):**
- Prerequisite: Comfortable running 25-30 miles per week with a longest run of 10+ miles
- Build long run progressively to 18-22 miles
- Include marathon-pace segments in long runs during peak weeks
- Peak mileage typically 3-4 weeks before race day

## Tapering Before a Race

Tapering means reducing training volume before race day so your body can fully recover and perform at its best. Most runners benefit from a 10-14 day taper for a half marathon and a 2-3 week taper for a marathon.

During taper: reduce overall mileage by 40-60% while maintaining some intensity. Keep one or two short speed sessions to maintain muscle tension and leg turnover — just shorten them significantly. Do not try new workouts, foods, or gear during taper week.

## Running Cadence and Form

The often-cited "180 steps per minute" target originated from coach Jack Daniels' observations of elite runners racing at fast paces. In reality, optimal cadence varies by individual, pace, height, and terrain, typically falling between 160-190 spm.

Rather than chasing a specific number, focus on these form cues:
- **Land with your foot under your hips**, not out in front (overstriding increases braking forces and injury risk)
- **Slight forward lean** from the ankles, not the waist
- **Relaxed shoulders and arms** swinging forward and back, not across the body
- **Quick, light ground contact** — think "running on hot coals"

If your cadence is below 160, gradually increase by 5% every 2-3 weeks. Use a metronome app during easy runs to practice.

## Cross-Training for Runners

**Strength Training** — 2-3 sessions per week focusing on single-leg exercises (lunges, step-ups, single-leg deadlifts), hip and glute strength (bridges, clamshells), core stability (planks, dead bugs, Pallof presses), and calf raises. Stronger muscles absorb impact better and maintain form when fatigued.

**Cycling** — Excellent low-impact option that builds aerobic fitness without the pounding. Great for recovery days or when building overall training volume.

**Swimming/Pool Running** — Zero-impact options that maintain cardiovascular fitness during injury recovery.

## Combining Running and Strength Training

Schedule hard running and hard strength on the same day (with at least 6 hours between if possible), leaving easy days truly easy. Running first is generally better when running performance is the priority. Keep strength sessions focused on compound movements and moderate volume — you're training to support running, not to compete in powerlifting. During peak running weeks, reduce strength volume but maintain intensity (fewer sets, same weight).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Running too fast on easy days** — The number one mistake. Easy means easy.
2. **Increasing mileage too quickly** — Follow the 10% rule as a guideline for weekly volume increases.
3. **Skipping the base phase** — Jumping straight into speed work invites injury.
4. **Ignoring strength training** — Runners who only run get injured more.
5. **Racing every weekend** — Hard efforts need recovery. Not every run is a race.
6. **Copying elite training plans** — What works for a professional with 10 years of base won't work for someone running 20 miles a week.
7. **Neglecting sleep and nutrition** — Training is stress. You adapt during recovery, not during the workout itself.
8. **No periodization** — Training should have phases: base, build, peak, taper. Running the same workouts year-round leads to stagnation and burnout.
