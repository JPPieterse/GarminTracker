# Nutrition Fundamentals for Active People

> Module for ZEV AI coaching knowledge base

## Macronutrient Basics

Every food you eat is made up of three macronutrients, each with a distinct job:

**Protein** builds and repairs muscle tissue, supports immune function, and helps you feel full. Each gram provides 4 calories. Best sources: chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, legumes, tofu.

**Carbohydrates** are your body's preferred fuel source, especially during moderate-to-high intensity exercise. Each gram provides 4 calories. Best sources: oats, rice, potatoes, fruit, whole-grain bread.

**Fats** support hormone production (including testosterone), absorb fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), and protect your joints. Each gram provides 9 calories. Best sources: olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish, eggs.

A solid starting framework for active people: 25-35% of calories from protein, 35-50% from carbs, and 20-35% from fat. Adjust based on goals, training style, and personal preference.

## Protein Requirements for Active People

The International Society of Sports Nutrition recommends 1.4-2.0 g of protein per kg of body weight per day for exercising individuals. Here is how to narrow that range:

- **General fitness / recreational exercise:** 1.4-1.6 g/kg
- **Endurance athletes (runners, cyclists):** 1.2-1.6 g/kg
- **Strength and power athletes:** 1.6-2.2 g/kg
- **During a calorie deficit (cutting):** push toward 2.0-2.2 g/kg to preserve muscle

For an 80 kg person doing regular resistance training, that means roughly 130-175 g of protein per day. Intakes above 2.2 g/kg show no additional muscle-building benefit for most people. Spreading protein across 3-4 meals with at least 25-40 g per serving optimizes muscle protein synthesis throughout the day.

## Protein Timing: The Anabolic Window Myth vs Reality

The old advice was to slam a protein shake within 30 minutes of your last set or lose your gains. Research has thoroughly debunked this narrow window. The real "anabolic window" is roughly 4-6 hours around your workout, not 30 minutes.

What actually matters most is total daily protein intake. Multiple meta-analyses confirm that hitting your daily protein target is a far stronger predictor of muscle growth than when exactly you eat it. That said, a practical approach is to have a protein-containing meal 1-3 hours before training and another within 1-3 hours after. If you train fasted first thing in the morning, getting protein in sooner after training becomes more relevant.

## Pre-Workout Nutrition

What you eat before training fuels performance. The key variable is timing:

- **2-3 hours before:** A balanced meal with carbs, protein, and moderate fat. Example: chicken breast with rice and vegetables, or oatmeal with eggs.
- **1-2 hours before:** A lighter snack emphasizing carbs with some protein. Example: banana with peanut butter, or Greek yogurt with berries.
- **30-60 minutes before:** Keep it liquid or easily digestible. Example: a smoothie, a piece of fruit, or a sports drink.

Carbohydrates before exercise allow you to train longer and at higher intensity. Avoid high-fat or high-fiber foods close to training as they slow digestion and can cause discomfort.

## Post-Workout Nutrition

After training, your goals are to replenish glycogen (energy stores) and provide amino acids for muscle repair. A meal or shake containing both protein (25-40 g) and carbohydrates (0.5-1.0 g/kg body weight) within a couple hours of training is ideal.

Good post-workout options: protein shake with a banana, chicken with rice, or eggs on toast. The post-workout meal does not need to be immediate unless you trained fasted or have another session coming within a few hours.

## Meal Frequency: Does It Matter?

Short answer: not nearly as much as total daily intake. Research consistently shows little difference in fat loss outcomes whether you eat 2 meals or 6 meals a day, as long as total calories and protein are matched.

For muscle building, spreading protein across 3-4 meals per day appears slightly better than cramming it all into 1-2 meals, because there is a ceiling on how much protein your body can use for muscle protein synthesis per meal (roughly 0.4-0.55 g/kg per sitting).

Practical guidance: eat 3-4 meals per day, each containing a good protein source. If you prefer 2 larger meals, that works too. The "eat every 2-3 hours to stoke your metabolism" advice is a myth. Total intake over the day is king.

## Hydration Guidelines

Dehydration of just 2% body weight can impair performance. General guidelines for active people:

- **Daily baseline:** roughly 35 ml per kg of body weight (about 2.8 L for an 80 kg person)
- **During exercise:** 400-800 ml per hour depending on sweat rate and conditions
- **Post-exercise:** replace 150% of fluid lost (if you lost 1 kg during a session, drink 1.5 L over the next few hours)
- **Electrolytes matter** for sessions over 60 minutes or in hot conditions. Sodium is the most important electrolyte to replace.

Monitor hydration by urine color: pale yellow is ideal. Dark yellow means drink more. Clear and frequent means you may be overdoing it.

## Practical Meal Planning for Busy People

Consistency beats perfection. These strategies help:

- **Batch cook proteins** on Sunday: grill chicken, cook ground turkey, boil eggs. Store in containers for the week.
- **Keep staples stocked:** rice, oats, frozen vegetables, canned beans, eggs, Greek yogurt, and frozen berries always in the kitchen.
- **Use the plate method:** half the plate vegetables, a quarter protein, a quarter starchy carbs. Simple and effective without counting anything.
- **Prep grab-and-go options:** overnight oats, protein shakes, hard-boiled eggs, trail mix.
- **When eating out:** prioritize a protein source, ask for vegetables, do not stress about the rest.

Tracking macros with an app works well for some people but is not required. If you find it stressful or obsessive, the plate method and consistent habits will get you 80% of the results.

## Supplements: What Works vs What Doesn't

**Tier 1 -- Strong evidence, worth considering:**

- **Creatine monohydrate:** The most well-researched supplement in sports nutrition. Improves strength, power output, and muscle growth. Take 3-5 g daily, every day, no loading phase needed. Safe for long-term use. Stick with creatine monohydrate, not fancy forms.
- **Protein powder (whey, casein, or plant-based):** Not magic, just a convenient way to hit your protein target. Use it when whole food is not practical.
- **Vitamin D:** Many people are deficient, especially those who spend most of their time indoors. Get blood levels tested. If below 30 ng/mL, supplement with 1000-4000 IU daily. Important for bone health, immune function, and potentially muscle function.
- **Caffeine:** 3-6 mg/kg body weight 30-60 minutes before training improves endurance and strength performance. Coffee counts.

**Tier 2 -- Moderate evidence or situational:**

- **Omega-3 fish oil:** supports recovery and joint health. Useful if you do not eat fatty fish 2-3 times per week.
- **Magnesium:** many active people are low. May help with sleep quality and muscle cramps. 200-400 mg before bed.

**Tier 3 -- Weak evidence or waste of money:**

- **BCAAs:** unnecessary if you are eating enough total protein. Your whole-food protein already contains these amino acids.
- **Fat burners / thermogenics:** mostly caffeine with marketing. Skip them.
- **Testosterone boosters:** no over-the-counter supplement meaningfully raises testosterone. Save your money.
- **Most "greens powders":** eat actual vegetables instead.

The supplement industry thrives on hype. Focus on diet first. Creatine and protein powder are the only two performance supplements most people will ever need.
