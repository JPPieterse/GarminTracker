# Weight Loss: Evidence-Based Strategies

> Module for ZEV AI coaching knowledge base

## Energy Balance Fundamentals

Weight loss comes down to one principle: consume fewer calories than you burn over time. No diet, food combination, or meal timing trick overrides this. Keto, intermittent fasting, low-fat -- they all work through the same mechanism: helping you eat fewer calories. The best approach is the one you can stick with.

Both sides of the equation are dynamic. Your body adjusts energy expenditure in response to changes in intake, activity, and body weight. Understanding these adjustments is key to long-term success.

## How to Calculate Maintenance Calories (TDEE)

Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is made up of four components:

- **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR):** calories burned at complete rest, roughly 60-70% of total expenditure
- **Thermic Effect of Food (TEF):** energy used to digest food, roughly 10% of intake
- **Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (EAT):** calories from intentional exercise
- **Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT):** calories from all other movement -- walking, fidgeting, standing, household tasks

To estimate TDEE, multiply body weight in kg by 28-35 depending on activity level (28 for sedentary, 35 for very active). Online calculators (Mifflin-St Jeor equation) provide a starting estimate, but for real accuracy, track food intake and body weight for 2-3 weeks. If weight is stable, your average intake equals maintenance.

## Sustainable Deficit Size

For most people, a daily deficit of 300-500 calories produces steady fat loss (roughly 0.3-0.5 kg per week) while preserving muscle and maintaining energy. This translates to about 0.5-1.0% of body weight lost per week.

People with more body fat to lose can tolerate a larger deficit (up to 1% of body weight per week) without excessive muscle loss. Leaner individuals should use smaller deficits (0.5% per week or less) to protect muscle mass. Aggressive deficits beyond this increase the risk of muscle loss, hormonal disruption, and binge-restrict cycles.

## Why Crash Diets Fail

Severe calorie restriction (eating 1000 calories or less, or cutting intake by 50%+) triggers a cascade of problems:

**Metabolic adaptation:** Your body reduces energy expenditure beyond what the weight loss alone would explain. Resting metabolic rate drops, NEAT decreases unconsciously (you fidget less, move less, take fewer steps), and the thermic effect of food shrinks. Research on Biggest Loser contestants found metabolic adaptation persisting 6 years after their rapid weight loss.

**Muscle loss:** In a severe deficit, the body breaks down muscle for energy. One study found participants in aggressive calorie restriction lost 60% more muscle mass than those in a moderate deficit. Muscle is metabolically active tissue -- losing it further lowers your metabolism.

**Hormonal disruption:** Crash dieting suppresses leptin (your satiety hormone), elevates ghrelin (your hunger hormone), reduces testosterone, and increases cortisol. This hormonal environment can persist for months after the diet ends, driving powerful hunger and fat regain.

**Psychological rebound:** Extreme restriction leads to food obsession, binge eating, and an all-or-nothing mindset. Most crash dieters regain all lost weight within 1-2 years, often ending heavier than they started.

## Tracking Food Intake

Tracking works, but accuracy matters. Common approaches:

- **Food logging apps (MyFitnessPal, MacroFactor, Cronometer):** weigh food with a kitchen scale. Eyeballing underestimates intake by 30-50%.
- **Hand-size portions:** palm = protein, fist = carbs, thumb = fat. Less precise but sustainable long-term.
- **Photo logging:** photograph every meal for accountability, even without counting.

Track for 4-8 weeks to build portion awareness, then transition to intuitive eating with periodic check-ins.

## Plateaus: Why They Happen and How to Break Through

Weight loss is not linear. Plateaus happen for several reasons:

- **Metabolic adaptation** reduces TDEE as you lose weight (a smaller body burns fewer calories)
- **NEAT decreases** unconsciously during a deficit -- research shows NEAT can drop by 150+ calories per day
- **Water retention** masks fat loss, especially during high-stress periods, menstrual cycles, increased carb intake, or new exercise programs
- **Tracking drift** -- portions creep up over time without you noticing

Before assuming a true plateau, confirm you are actually in one: weight has been flat for 2-3 weeks while adherence has been consistent. If so, your options are:

1. **Reduce intake by another 100-200 calories** (do not go below reasonable minimums)
2. **Increase NEAT** -- add 2000-3000 daily steps
3. **Take a diet break** (see below) and restart with fresh adherence
4. **Re-verify tracking accuracy** -- weigh food again, audit entries

## The Role of NEAT

NEAT is arguably the most underappreciated factor in weight management. Differences in NEAT between individuals can account for up to 2000 calories per day. During a calorie deficit, your body unconsciously reduces NEAT -- you fidget less, stand less, take fewer steps, and generally move less throughout the day.

This is why step tracking matters during a cut. Maintaining a daily step target (8000-12000 steps) counteracts the unconscious NEAT decline and keeps your total energy expenditure higher. Walking is the simplest, most sustainable tool for increasing energy expenditure without adding training stress or increasing appetite.

## Diet Breaks and Refeed Days

**Diet breaks** are planned periods (typically 1-2 weeks) where you eat at maintenance calories. Research shows they help preserve resting metabolic rate, maintain muscle mass, improve adherence, and restore leptin levels. A practical approach: after every 4-8 weeks of dieting, take a 1-2 week break at maintenance.

**Refeed days** are shorter (1-3 days) where you increase calories to maintenance or slightly above, primarily through extra carbohydrates. Refeeds temporarily boost leptin and replenish muscle glycogen, improving training performance and mood. One to two refeed days per week can help during longer dieting phases.

Both strategies are supported by research showing intermittent energy restriction is at least as effective as continuous restriction for fat loss, with better preservation of muscle mass and metabolic rate.

## Behavioral Strategies for Long-Term Adherence

Fat loss is a behavioral challenge more than a knowledge problem. Strategies that work:

- **Environment design:** keep trigger foods out of the house. Stock visible, easy-to-grab healthy options.
- **Protein and fiber first:** prioritize protein and vegetables at every meal. They are the most satiating macronutrients per calorie.
- **Plan for imperfection:** one bad meal does not ruin a week. One bad week does not ruin a month. The goal is consistency, not perfection.
- **Sleep and stress management:** poor sleep increases hunger hormones and cravings. Manage stress to prevent emotional eating.
- **Accountability:** whether through a coach, a friend, or a food log, external accountability dramatically improves adherence.
- **Flexible dieting:** no foods are "off limits." Fitting treats into your calorie budget prevents the restrict-binge cycle.

## Progress Tracking: Trends Over Daily Fluctuations

Body weight fluctuates 1-3 kg daily due to water, sodium, carb intake, bowel contents, and hormonal shifts. Weighing daily but looking at the weekly average is the best approach. Use a trend-tracking app or simply average your 7 daily weights each week.

Additional markers: progress photos (monthly, same lighting), waist measurements (biweekly), how clothes fit, strength trends, and energy levels. If the weekly average trends down over 3-4 weeks, you are losing fat regardless of any single day's number.

## When to Stop Cutting

Not every phase should be a deficit. Signs it is time to transition to maintenance:

- You have reached your goal body fat level or physique
- You have been dieting for 12-16 weeks continuously
- Training performance is declining significantly
- Sleep quality is deteriorating
- Mood, libido, or energy are consistently low
- You are experiencing persistent hunger that willpower cannot manage

When transitioning out of a deficit, increase calories gradually (add 100-200 calories per week) back to maintenance rather than jumping straight up. This "reverse diet" approach helps prevent rapid fat regain and allows your metabolism to recover. Expect some scale weight increase from water and glycogen -- this is not fat gain. Spend at least 4-8 weeks at maintenance before starting another cut if needed.
