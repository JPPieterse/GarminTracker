# Heart Rate Training

> Module for ZEV AI coaching knowledge base

## Heart Rate Zones Explained

Heart rate training divides effort into five zones based on percentage of your maximum heart rate (max HR). Each zone targets different physiological systems and produces different adaptations.

| Zone | Name | % of Max HR | Description |
|------|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Recovery | 50-60% | Very light effort. Walking or very slow jogging. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery days. |
| 2 | Aerobic Base | 60-70% | Easy, sustainable effort. You can hold a full conversation. This is where you build your aerobic engine and burn the highest percentage of fat for fuel. |
| 3 | Tempo | 70-80% | Moderate effort. You can speak in short sentences. Sometimes called the "gray zone" because it's hard enough to fatigue you but not intense enough for maximum adaptation. |
| 4 | Threshold | 80-90% | Hard effort. You can only manage a few words. This is interval and race-pace territory for most runners. Develops VO2 max and speed. |
| 5 | Maximum | 90-100% | All-out effort. Sustainable for only 30 seconds to 3 minutes. Develops anaerobic capacity and peak speed. |

## How to Estimate Max Heart Rate

The classic formula — 220 minus your age — is convenient but inaccurate. It can be off by 10-20 beats per minute for any given individual. It was never intended as a precise tool; it's a rough population average with a large standard deviation.

**Better estimation formulas:**
- **Tanaka formula:** 208 - (0.7 x age) — More accurate, especially for adults over 40
- **Gulati formula (women):** 206 - (0.88 x age) — Developed specifically for women, since the standard formulas tend to overestimate female max HR
- **HUNT formula (from Norwegian research):** 211 - (0.64 x age) — Based on a large population study

**The best method:** A field test. After a thorough warm-up, run a hard uphill effort for 3-4 minutes, jog for 3 minutes, then repeat with maximum effort. Your peak heart rate in the second interval is a reliable estimate of your max HR. Do this when you're rested, healthy, and motivated. Or, accumulate data over several months of hard training sessions and races — your highest recorded value is likely close to your true max.

All formulas are estimates. Your actual max HR is genetically determined, does not improve with fitness, and decreases slowly with age. Two people of the same age can have max heart rates 20+ beats apart.

## Resting Heart Rate

Resting heart rate (RHR) is the number of times your heart beats per minute when you're completely at rest. For most adults, normal RHR falls between 60-100 bpm, but fit individuals often see 40-60 bpm or even lower.

**What to watch for:**
- A gradually declining RHR over months of consistent training is a sign of improving cardiovascular fitness
- A sudden spike in RHR (5-10+ beats above your baseline) may indicate illness, overtraining, dehydration, poor sleep, or high stress
- Track your RHR first thing in the morning before getting out of bed for the most consistent readings
- Trends matter more than any single reading

## Training in Zone 2

Zone 2 has become the most discussed training zone in endurance sports for good reason. It sits at the intersection of sustainable effort and maximum aerobic development.

**What Zone 2 training does:**
- Builds mitochondrial density — more energy factories in your muscle cells
- Improves fat oxidation — teaches your body to burn fat as primary fuel, sparing glycogen
- Increases capillary density — better oxygen delivery to working muscles
- Strengthens the heart — improves stroke volume (more blood pumped per beat)
- Supports recovery — low enough stress to train frequently without breaking down

**How it should feel:** You can hold a full conversation. You could sustain this effort for hours. It feels embarrassingly slow, especially for competitive people. That's normal and correct.

**How much:** For endurance athletes, 70-80% of total training volume should be in zones 1-2, with most of that in zone 2. This typically means 3-5 easy runs per week for runners, or the majority of cycling hours for cyclists.

## When to Use High-Intensity Zones

High-intensity work (zones 4-5) is the other 20% of the equation. It produces adaptations that zone 2 cannot: improved VO2 max, lactate clearance, neuromuscular coordination, and running economy.

**Best applications:**
- **Intervals at zone 4-5:** 1-2 sessions per week during build and peak phases
- **Tempo runs at zone 3-4:** Sustained threshold work to raise lactate threshold
- **Race-pace sessions:** Teaching your body to sustain goal race effort

High-intensity sessions need adequate recovery — typically 48 hours before the next hard effort. Never stack multiple hard days without easy days between them unless you're an advanced athlete following a deliberate overreaching protocol.

## Cardiac Drift

Cardiac drift is the gradual increase in heart rate during prolonged steady-state exercise, even though your pace and perceived effort remain constant. During a 90-minute run at the same pace, your heart rate might climb 10-15 beats from start to finish.

**Why it happens:**
- Core body temperature rises, requiring more blood flow to the skin for cooling
- Sweat causes fluid loss, reducing blood volume
- Stroke volume decreases slightly as blood is redirected, so heart rate rises to maintain cardiac output

**What it means for training:**
- An increase of up to 5% in heart rate over 60+ minutes is normal and indicates good aerobic fitness
- A drift significantly above 5% suggests you started too fast, your aerobic base needs work, or conditions (heat, humidity) are challenging
- On long runs, accept some drift rather than slowing to stay in a specific heart rate zone — your body is doing what it should
- As your aerobic fitness improves over months and years, cardiac drift decreases

## Heart Rate Recovery

Heart rate recovery (HRR) measures how quickly your heart rate drops after stopping intense exercise. It's one of the most reliable field indicators of cardiovascular fitness.

**How to measure it:** Note your heart rate immediately after stopping a hard effort, then check again after exactly 1 minute. The difference is your 1-minute HRR.

**What the numbers mean:**
- **HRR greater than 30 bpm in 1 minute:** Excellent cardiovascular fitness
- **HRR of 20-30 bpm:** Good fitness
- **HRR of 12-20 bpm:** Average
- **HRR below 12 bpm:** Below average — may indicate poor fitness or overtraining

A faster recovery indicates a well-functioning parasympathetic nervous system (your body's "rest and recover" mode). Track HRR over time — improving recovery speed is a concrete sign of fitness gains.

## Using HR Data From Wearables

Wrist-based heart rate monitors are convenient but have limitations. They can lag behind actual heart rate during rapid changes (like interval starts), struggle during activities with wrist movement, and occasionally produce erratic readings.

**Best practices:**
- Use a chest strap for interval sessions and races where accuracy matters most
- Wrist-based HR is generally reliable for easy and steady-state runs
- Look at averages and trends, not moment-to-moment fluctuations
- Set heart rate alerts for your zone boundaries to get real-time feedback
- Compare heart rate data alongside pace and perceived effort — when all three align, you have a complete picture

**When HR data conflicts with how you feel:**
- If your heart rate seems high but you feel fine, check for sensor issues (tight strap, dry skin, poor contact)
- If your heart rate is higher than expected for a given pace on an easy day, consider fatigue, heat, dehydration, illness, or stress
- If heart rate is lower than expected during a hard effort, the sensor may be lagging or malfunctioning

## Morning Heart Rate Readings

Checking your heart rate first thing in the morning — before getting out of bed, after waking naturally — provides the most controlled, repeatable measurement you can get.

**What elevated morning HR tells you:**
- **3-5 bpm above baseline:** Minor — could be poor sleep, mild dehydration, or a stressful day ahead. Proceed normally but pay attention.
- **5-10 bpm above baseline:** Moderate — consider reducing training intensity. Your body may be fighting something or recovering from accumulated training load.
- **10+ bpm above baseline:** Significant — take a rest day or very easy session. This often precedes illness by 1-2 days, or indicates meaningful overreaching.

The key is establishing your personal baseline over 2-4 weeks of consistent morning measurements. The absolute number matters less than deviations from your norm.
