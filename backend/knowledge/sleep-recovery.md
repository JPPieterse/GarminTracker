# Sleep and Recovery for Athletes

> Module for ZEV AI coaching knowledge base

## Sleep Stages and What Happens in Each

Sleep cycles through distinct stages in roughly 90-minute periods throughout the night. Understanding what each stage does explains why total sleep duration and quality both matter.

**N1 (Light Sleep):** Brief transition from wake to sleep. Muscles relax, heart rate slows.

**N2 (Moderate Sleep):** About 50% of total sleep. Body temperature drops. Important for memory consolidation and motor learning.

**N3 (Deep Sleep / Slow-Wave Sleep):** Where physical recovery happens. The body's largest growth hormone surge occurs here, driving tissue repair, muscle growth, and bone strengthening. Concentrated in the first half of the night.

**REM Sleep:** Mental recovery stage. Consolidates memories, processes emotions, and builds neural connections for skill learning. Concentrated in the second half of the night -- cutting sleep short disproportionately reduces REM.

Key takeaway: you need a full night to get both deep sleep (physical repair) and REM (mental repair).

## How Much Sleep Athletes Actually Need

The general recommendation is 7-9 hours for adults, but athletes in regular training should aim for 8-9 hours as a minimum. During heavy training phases, 9-10 hours may be beneficial.

Why 6 hours is not enough: sleeping 6 hours or less impairs reaction time, strength, endurance, and decision-making. Chronic short sleep creates cumulative debt that compounds over weeks. People who regularly sleep 6 hours believe they have adapted, but performance testing shows they have not -- they have lost the ability to perceive their own impairment.

Sleep extension studies (increasing to 10 hours) show improvements in sprint times, accuracy, and mood. More sleep is one of the most underutilized performance tools.

## Sleep's Impact on Muscle Recovery and Hormones

Sleep is when your body does its heaviest repair work, driven by two key hormones:

**Growth Hormone (GH):** Up to 75% of daily growth hormone release occurs during deep sleep. GH stimulates muscle protein synthesis, tissue repair, and fat metabolism. Poor sleep blunts GH secretion substantially.

**Testosterone:** Testosterone follows a circadian pattern, rising during sleep and peaking in the early morning during REM sleep. Even a single night of sleep deprivation can reduce testosterone levels by roughly 24%. Chronically low sleep suppresses testosterone to a degree equivalent to aging 10-15 years.

Sleep deprivation also raises cortisol (a catabolic stress hormone) by approximately 21%, which promotes muscle breakdown and fat storage. The combined effect of reduced anabolic hormones and elevated cortisol shifts your body toward losing muscle and holding fat -- the opposite of what any athlete wants.

## Sleep's Impact on Fat Loss

Poor sleep does not just slow fat loss -- it changes what kind of weight you lose. A landmark study found that when participants slept 5.5 hours versus 8.5 hours during a calorie deficit, the short-sleep group lost 55% less fat and 60% more muscle mass. Same calorie deficit, drastically different results.

The mechanisms are clear: sleep deprivation increases ghrelin (hunger hormone), decreases leptin (satiety hormone), increases cravings for high-calorie foods, and impairs insulin sensitivity. People who are sleep-deprived eat an estimated 300-400 extra calories per day on average, largely from snacking.

If you are trying to lose fat, optimizing sleep may be the single highest-leverage thing you can do -- more impactful than tweaking macros or adding cardio.

## HRV: What It Means and How to Use It

Heart rate variability (HRV) measures the variation in time between heartbeats. Counterintuitively, higher variability is better -- it indicates a well-recovered, adaptable nervous system. Low HRV suggests stress, fatigue, or inadequate recovery.

**How to use HRV:**

- Measure every morning under consistent conditions (lying down, before coffee, same time)
- Track your baseline for 2-4 weeks before acting on it
- HRV within or above normal range: green light for hard training
- HRV below normal: lower intensity, active recovery, or rest
- Use 7-day rolling averages, not single-day readings

HRV is one data point, not the whole picture. Low readings could reflect poor sleep, alcohol, illness, or stress. Combine with subjective measures: how do you feel, how motivated are you? Wearables like Garmin, Whoop, Apple Watch, and Oura track HRV with reasonable accuracy for trends.

## Signs of Overtraining and Under-Recovery

Overtraining syndrome is rare in recreational athletes, but under-recovery is common. Watch for these warning signs:

- **Performance decline:** weights feel heavier, times get slower, progress stalls or reverses despite consistent training
- **Persistent fatigue:** feeling drained even after rest days and adequate sleep
- **Elevated resting heart rate:** 5+ bpm above your normal baseline for several days
- **Suppressed HRV:** consistently below your personal baseline
- **Mood changes:** irritability, lack of motivation, anxiety, or depression
- **Sleep disruption:** difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep despite being exhausted
- **Increased illness:** getting sick more frequently
- **Lingering soreness:** muscle soreness lasting well beyond 48-72 hours
- **Loss of appetite or unexplained weight loss**
- **Reduced libido**

If you notice 3 or more of these signs persisting for more than a week, it is time to pull back. The fix is not more training -- it is more recovery.

## Active Recovery Strategies

Rest days do not mean you have to be completely sedentary. Active recovery promotes blood flow, reduces stiffness, and supports the recovery process without adding training stress.

**Effective options:**

- **Walking:** 20-40 minutes easy pace. Best recovery tool -- promotes blood flow, zero recovery cost.
- **Light cycling or swimming:** 20-30 minutes at conversational pace (HR under 120 bpm).
- **Mobility work:** foam rolling, stretching, yoga. Focus on tight or restricted areas.
- **Light bodyweight movement:** stretching circuits, band work at very low intensity.

If your recovery session makes you sore, it was too intense. The goal is restorative, not challenging.

## Deload Weeks: When and How

A deload week is a planned reduction in training volume or intensity, typically every 4-8 weeks. It allows accumulated fatigue to dissipate so you can come back stronger.

**When:** Every 4-6 weeks for experienced lifters, 6-8 weeks for intermediates, or reactively when you see accumulated fatigue signs. Always after competitions.

**How:**

- **Reduce volume:** same weights, cut sets by 40-50%
- **Reduce intensity:** same sets/reps at 50-60% of normal weight
- **Reduce both:** best when feeling genuinely beaten up

Maintain your normal schedule -- show up but leave feeling fresh. Deloads are when adaptation occurs. Many lifters hit PRs in the week following a proper deload.

## Napping: Benefits and Timing

Naps can be a powerful recovery tool when used correctly.

**Benefits for athletes:**

- A 20-30 minute nap improves alertness, reaction time, and mood without causing grogginess
- Naps partially offset the effects of a poor night's sleep
- They provide an additional window for growth hormone release

**Timing guidelines:**

- **Best window:** early to mid-afternoon (1:00-3:00 PM), aligning with the natural post-lunch dip in alertness
- **Duration:** 20-30 minutes for a quick recharge without entering deep sleep. If you have time for a full 90-minute nap, that allows a complete sleep cycle and can be highly restorative.
- **Avoid napping after 4:00 PM** -- it can interfere with nighttime sleep onset
- **Avoid 40-60 minute naps** -- you wake during deep sleep and feel worse (sleep inertia)

Naps are a supplement to nighttime sleep, not a replacement. If you consistently need long naps to function, your nighttime sleep needs attention.

## Sleep Hygiene Tips That Actually Matter

Not all sleep tips are equally impactful. Focus on these high-leverage habits:

**The big three (highest impact):**

1. **Consistent sleep/wake times:** within a 30-minute window every day, including weekends. This anchors your circadian rhythm more than anything else.
2. **Dark, cool room:** blackout curtains or sleep mask, 18-20 degrees Celsius. Your body must drop core temperature to initiate sleep.
3. **No screens 30-60 minutes before bed:** blue light suppresses melatonin. Use night mode if you must, but a book or podcast is better.

**Also important:**

- **Caffeine cutoff:** none after 2:00 PM. Half the caffeine from a 3 PM coffee is still active at 9 PM.
- **Alcohol disrupts sleep architecture:** reduces REM and increases awakenings. Sedation is not real sleep.
- **Exercise improves sleep** -- but avoid intense training within 2-3 hours of bedtime.
- **Wind-down routine:** 15-30 minutes of consistent calming activities signals your brain that sleep is coming.

If you are doing everything right and still struggling, track sleep with a wearable and consult a provider if issues persist beyond 2-3 weeks.
