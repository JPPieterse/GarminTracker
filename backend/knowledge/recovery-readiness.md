# Recovery & Readiness to Train

> Module for ZEV AI coaching knowledge base

## Heart Rate Variability (HRV) — The Recovery Indicator

HRV measures the variation in time between heartbeats. Higher HRV generally indicates better recovery and readiness to train. Lower HRV suggests the body is under stress and may need more recovery.

### Interpreting HRV Trends

- **Your baseline matters more than any single number.** HRV is highly individual — one person's 45ms is another person's 85ms. Track your 7-day rolling average.
- **A drop of 10-15% below your baseline** for 2+ consecutive days suggests accumulated fatigue. Consider an easy day or rest.
- **A single low reading** isn't cause for alarm — poor sleep, alcohol, late meals, and stress all temporarily lower HRV.
- **Consistently rising HRV over weeks** indicates improving fitness and recovery capacity.
- **Morning readings are most reliable** — measure before getting out of bed, before coffee.

### HRV and Training Decisions

| HRV Status | What It Means | Training Recommendation |
|------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Above baseline | Well recovered | Green light for hard training |
| At baseline | Normal recovery | Train as planned |
| 5-10% below baseline | Mild fatigue | Train but reduce intensity slightly |
| 10-15% below for 2+ days | Accumulated fatigue | Easy session or active recovery |
| 15%+ below for 3+ days | Significant stress | Rest day, check sleep/nutrition/life stress |

## Resting Heart Rate as a Recovery Marker

- **Normal daily fluctuation:** ±3-5 bpm is normal and doesn't indicate anything.
- **Elevated by 5-10 bpm for multiple days:** Possible overreaching, illness coming on, or accumulated stress.
- **Sudden spike of 10+ bpm:** Check for illness, dehydration, poor sleep, or significant life stress. Not a training day.
- **Trending downward over months:** Improving cardiovascular fitness — a great sign.
- **Trending upward over weeks despite training:** Possible overtraining or insufficient recovery.

## Recovery Time Between Sessions

### Muscle Group Recovery

- **Large muscle groups** (legs, back): 48-72 hours between heavy sessions
- **Small muscle groups** (arms, shoulders): 24-48 hours
- **The same muscle group** at high intensity should have at least 48 hours rest
- **Light/moderate work** on a muscle group can be done with 24 hours rest

### System Recovery

- **Central nervous system (CNS):** Heavy compound lifts (deadlifts, squats at 85%+ 1RM) tax the CNS beyond just muscle fatigue. May need 72+ hours before another heavy session.
- **Cardiovascular system:** Easy runs (zone 1-2) can be done daily. High-intensity intervals need 48+ hours between sessions.
- **Combined training:** Running + lifting in the same week requires careful scheduling. Don't do heavy legs and hard intervals on back-to-back days.

## Body Battery and Daily Readiness

Garmin's Body Battery combines HRV, stress, sleep, and activity data into a 0-100 score.

- **75-100:** Fully charged — great day for a hard session
- **50-75:** Moderate energy — train as planned but listen to your body
- **25-50:** Low energy — consider an easy session or focus on technique/mobility
- **0-25:** Depleted — rest or very light movement only

### Patterns to Watch

- **Not charging above 60 overnight:** Sleep quality issue — check sleep hygiene, stress, late training, or screen time
- **Draining rapidly during the day without exercise:** High stress day — adjust training expectations
- **Consistently low despite adequate sleep:** Possible overtraining, illness incubation, or chronic stress

## Sleep Quality and Recovery

- **Deep sleep is when physical recovery happens.** Less than 45 minutes of deep sleep significantly impairs muscle recovery.
- **REM sleep handles mental recovery.** Athletes need adequate REM for motor learning and coordination.
- **Sleep consistency matters more than duration.** Same bedtime ±30 minutes outperforms variable sleep even if total hours are the same.
- **One night of poor sleep** won't ruin a workout. Two or more consecutive nights will measurably impact performance.

## The Readiness Decision Framework

Before each training session, consider these signals together:

1. **How did you sleep?** (Duration + quality from wearable)
2. **What's your morning resting HR?** (Compared to your baseline)
3. **What's your Body Battery?** (Above or below 50?)
4. **How do you feel?** (Subjective feel matters — sometimes the watch is wrong)
5. **What did you do yesterday?** (Heavy session = your body is still recovering)

**Rule of thumb:** If 3+ of these signals say "go easy," listen. One bad signal can be ignored. Multiple bad signals together are your body telling you something.

## When to Push Through vs When to Back Off

**Push through when:**
- You're just not "feeling it" but all metrics are fine
- Mild muscle soreness (DOMS) from previous session
- First 10 minutes feel rough but you start warming up

**Back off when:**
- Elevated resting HR + poor sleep + low Body Battery
- Sharp pain (not just soreness) during movement
- Feeling genuinely ill — sore throat, body aches, fever
- You've had 3+ hard sessions in a row without a rest day
- Life stress is exceptionally high (move, deadline, relationship stress)
